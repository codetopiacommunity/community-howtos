# Ways to Contribute

Contributing to Codetopia Community is not just about writing code.
Whatever your skills, there is a place for you here. This guide walks you
through every way you can get involved.

> **All roles in the community are voluntary and unpaid**, including
> department leads. What you gain is experience, mentorship, and a
> public track record of your work.

---
## Who can contribute?

**On GitHub: anyone can help.** Our repos are public. If you spot a bug,
a typo, a broken link, or something that no longer works the way the docs
say it does, you can report it or fix it yourself. You do not need to be
a member to make something better.

**Beyond that, you need to be a member.** Joining a department, writing
for our blog, mentoring, volunteering at events, and writing new howtos
are all for verified members of the community. These need context that
only comes from being part of what we are building.

**Contributing on GitHub but not a member yet?** Join us. You will be able
to talk directly with maintainers, have your work recognized inside the
community, and unlock every other way to contribute on this page. It takes
a few minutes.

## Quick contributions

These are things you can do today, no long-term commitment needed.

### Fix something on GitHub

Spotted a typo in our docs? A broken link? A bug on the website? You can
report it or fix it yourself, even if you have never contributed to a
project before. Our guides walk you through it step by step.

### Write something

Writing comes in two forms, and you can do either or both. They are
different jobs, so here is how to tell them apart.

**Howtos** are practical guides and walkthroughs for getting things done
in Codetopia Community. How to join, how to use Discord, how to
contribute to a project, how to submit your work. If a member is stuck on
something about how this community works, a howto is what unblocks them.
The guide you are reading right now is one. They live in our
[community-howtos](https://github.com/codetopiacommunity/community-howtos)
repo.

- **Who writes them?** Members, mostly. Writing a good howto means
  knowing what people around here actually get stuck on, and that comes
  from being part of the community.
- **Can anyone fix one?** Yes. If you spot a typo, a broken link, or a
  step that no longer matches what you see on screen, open a pull
  request. You do not need to be a member to make something better.
- **How?** Through GitHub, the same way as code.
- **What happens next?** We review it, and once it is merged it becomes
  part of this collection.

**Still not sure which one you are writing?** Ask yourself who is reading
and why. If they are stuck and want it fixed, it is a howto. If they
are curious and want to understand, it is an article.
**Technical articles** are teaching. They answer "help me understand
this" rather than "how do I do this." Tutorials, deep dives, lessons
learned, things you figured out the hard way. People read them to learn
something, not to unblock themselves in the next five minutes. They are
longer, they carry your voice, and they are published on our Hashnode
blog under Codetopia Community's name.
